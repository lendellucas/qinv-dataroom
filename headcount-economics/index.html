1. Executive summary

A large wealth manager spends the majority of its cost base on people, and most of that labor sits in repeatable, rules-based operational work: research, reporting, reconciliation, onboarding, and service. QINV is built to absorb exactly that work. Fully integrated, it does not trim a team, it removes the need for the team to scale with assets.

Wealth and private-banking businesses run at a 60–80% cost-to-income ratio, and compensation is typically 50–65% of total cost [Industry-reported: Oliver Wyman / Deloitte wealth benchmarks, 2023-2024]. The single largest lever on margin is therefore operational headcount per dollar of AUM. QINV's architecture, 15 modules and 150+ coordinated AI agents across Intelligence, Operations and Distribution, is designed to break the link between asset growth and headcount growth.

2. Why wealth management is structurally labor-heavy

For every revenue-generating advisor, a wealth franchise carries a stack of operational staff who never touch a client portfolio's strategy but are essential to running it: trade support, reconciliation, performance reporting, account opening, KYC refresh, suitability, document production, surveillance, and client service.

The structural point

In a traditional firm, doubling AUM means roughly doubling the operational stack. With QINV fully integrated, the agent layer absorbs the marginal operational load, so assets can grow while operating headcount stays flat. That is the difference between a linear cost business and a scalable one.

Honest distinction: "work automatable" is not the same as "jobs cut." A large share of freed capacity is typically reinvested into growth, advisor support, and new services. Our model converts only a fraction of automatable work into actual cost takeout (see realization factor below), which is why our headline numbers sit well below the raw automation ceiling.

5. The headcount-economics model

Three steps, all transparent:

  1. Blended automatable share = sum of (function share × automatable %) across all operational functions.
  2. Realization factor = the fraction of automatable work converted into real cost/FTE takeout. We use 0.35 / 0.55 / 0.75 for conservative / base / aggressive. The remainder is reinvested capacity, not savings.
  3. Net operating-headcount reduction = blended automatable share × realization factor, applied to the operational (non-advisor) headcount, valued at a region-specific fully-loaded cost per FTE.

Reading the numbers

Even in the conservative scenario, the annual operating saving for a single mega-client (Morgan Stanley WM, UBS GWM) exceeds $700M. At base case it clears $1.5bn. For the Brazilian leaders, base-case savings of $97M (XP) and $135M (BTG) dwarf any conceivable platform fee, which is the entire commercial argument.

7. Why this is defensible in diligence

  • Advisors are never cut. Only operational, middle/back-office, reporting, onboarding, service and compliance-support staff are modeled. Revenue-facing capacity is protected.
  • Realization factor is conservative. We bank only 35–75% of automatable work as savings; the rest is reinvested capacity. This avoids the classic overstatement of "automatable = eliminated."
  • Third-party benchmarks, not self-assessment. Automation rates come from McKinsey, BCG, Citi GPS and Evident, not from QINV.
  • Public anchor figures. Client AUM, advisor counts and headcount come from filings and investor materials.
  • Ranges, not single numbers. Every output is a three-scenario band, so the thesis does not depend on a single optimistic input.
